Vision

The vision describes what we want to achieve: the clear, concise and inspirational long-term desired change resulting from your digital service, or programme’s work.

This should be used to set a North Star to drive the direction of you and your teams.

A good vision should include:

  • where are we going?
  • what do we want to achieve in the future?
  • what kind of future do we envisage?
  • an outcome, not a solution

It should be:

  • clear
  • aspirational (yet achievable)
  • stretching
  • memorable
  • concise
  • inspiring
  • linked to strategic goals/mission statement
  • phase agnostic - use one vision with missions or goals for each product phase
  • active
  • possible to measure

Avoid:

  • complex language
  • scope being too broad
  • use of generic phrases
  • failing to convey a message
  • acronyms/specialist language
  • over-complication
  • stagnation
  • setting it aline - you should test your vision with your users, team and stakeholders

Mission

A mission is a clear, meaningful and motivational statement(s) that clarifies what you are seeking to achieve and who it is for. You can use this as a guiding policy for decision making – does the work contribute to the mission?

A good mission should include:

  • what do we do now?
  • who do we do it for?
  • what are we trying to accomplish to solve the problem?
  • what impact do we want to achieve?

It should be:

  • easily understandable about what will be achieved
  • inspirational for people
  • the elevator pitch
  • tangible
  • valuable

Avoid:

  • unrealistic goals, wooly objectives and goals not being SMART
  • scope being too broad
  • use of jargon and acronyms - it should be meaningful to those who read it
  • failing to convey a message

Strategy

A strategy describes the outcomes your team will use to realise the product vision – this is the "how".

Links measurements and metrics to a successful outcome of deployment of the strategy and product(s) – and considering what will and won’t be influenced by a digital product.

It should:

  • be outcome based
  • link to the bigger picture
  • cover product lifecycle, not just creation and development
  • include the 'ways' and 'means' to achieve the 'ends'
  • be written in plain English so anyone can understand it
  • understand what will make the biggest difference

Avoid:

  • creating the strategy in isolation
  • it being a delivery plan
  • a wordy document
  • the output being definitive, the strategy may change due to external factors

Considering the bigger picture

Know how your work fits within the overall goals of DfE, and the broader aims of digital government – and communicate to try and influence future strategies.

Understand the ecosystem of your work – what links to it, what sits near it. Find people working on similar services and learn from them.

Understand what standards you need to adhere to, and understand constraints - both in terms of scope and policy, and technical choices.

Develop and maintain an understanding of economic, social, political, environmental and technological developments to ensure activity is relevant

Bring together views, perspectives and diverse needs of users and stakeholders to gain a broader understanding of the space you are working in.
